\section{Context and Problem Statement}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Industrial Context}

Organizations operating on SAS face mounting pressure to migrate to modern, open-source Python stacks. The primary drivers are:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Migration Drivers}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Driver} & \textbf{Detail} \\
\midrule
Licensing cost & SAS enterprise licenses: \$8,000–\$100,000+ per seat/year. Python is free. \\
Ecosystem velocity & Python's ecosystem (pandas, scikit-learn, PyTorch, statsmodels) advances faster. \\
Talent availability & New graduates enter with Python skills, not SAS. \\
Infrastructure & Cloud-native architectures (AWS, GCP, Azure) are built around Python. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{infobox}[Scale of the Problem]
Manual translation costs 3–8 hours per 100 lines of SAS. For a 500,000-line codebase, this means \textbf{15,000–40,000 hours} of developer time — effectively years of effort.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Problem Statement — The Six Failure Modes}

The challenge is not syntactic translation — it is \textbf{semantic equivalence under complexity}. Six documented failure modes cause silent errors:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Six Critical Failure Modes in SAS$\to$Python Translation}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Failure Mode} & \textbf{Why It Breaks} \\
\midrule
\texttt{DATE\_ARITHMETIC} & SAS epoch: Jan 1, 1960 vs Python's 1970. Silent numerical errors in every date calculation. \\
\texttt{MERGE\_SEMANTICS} & SAS \texttt{MERGE} has sorted-join semantics fundamentally different from \texttt{pd.merge()}. \\
\texttt{RETAIN} & Creates persistent row-to-row state in DATA steps — no direct Python equivalent. \\
\texttt{FIRST./LAST.} & Automatic variables depend on BY-group processing absent in pandas. \\
\texttt{MISSING\_VALUE} & SAS numeric missing (\texttt{.}) behaves as $-\infty$ in comparisons; Python's \texttt{NaN} returns \texttt{False}. \\
\texttt{PROC\_MEANS\_OUTPUT} & \texttt{OUTPUT OUT=} produces a specific column structure that must be replicated exactly. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{Solution Approach}

An AI-powered, agentic SAS-to-Python conversion accelerator combining:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Core Approach Components}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
Semantic partitioning & RAPTOR-inspired hierarchical clustering (Sarthi et al., ICLR 2024) instead of flat keyword splitting. \\
KB-augmented translation & Three RAG paradigms: Static RAG (simple blocks, fixed $k$), GraphRAG (dependency-aware, NetworkX context), Agentic RAG (autonomous retrieval: adaptive $k$, query reformulation, level escalation, retrieval retry). \\
Calibrated QA & Cross-verification by second LLM call; quality metrics tracked to detect drift. \\
Structured reconstruction & Merged in execution order with consolidated imports, resolved variables, syntax validation. \\
Continuous learning & Corrections feed back into the KB automatically. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{Three RAG Paradigms}

The system uses three distinct retrieval-augmented generation strategies, each triggered by different conditions. This is not a single RAG pattern — each paradigm has its own retrieval policy, context construction, and escalation behavior.

\subsubsection{Static RAG — Simple Blocks}

\begin{infobox}[When: LOW risk, simple partition types (DATA\_STEP, PROC\_SQL, assignment)]
Fixed retrieval: \texttt{KBQueryClient.query\_similar(k=3)}. No failure mode filtering, no graph context. Retrieved pairs injected as few-shot context. LLM: Ollama 8B. No cross-verification, no retry. Fastest path.
\end{infobox}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Static RAG Pipeline}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} c l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Step} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Details} \\
\midrule
1 & Fixed retrieval & \texttt{query\_similar(k=3, level="leaf")}. No query reformulation. \\
2 & Inject & 3 SAS$\to$Python pairs as few-shot context. \\
3 & Generate & Ollama 8B, single pass. \\
4 & Accept & Output directly to L4 merge (no cross-verify). \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{GraphRAG — Dependency-Aware Blocks}

\begin{infobox}[When: Partition has cross-file dependencies, is part of an SCC group, or uses macros defined elsewhere]
Before querying the KB, the agent traverses the NetworkX dependency graph to collect \emph{structural context}: upstream partition translations (already converted), downstream consumers, and SCC siblings. This graph context is injected alongside KB pairs, giving the LLM visibility into the dependency chain.
\end{infobox}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{GraphRAG Pipeline}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} c l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Step} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Details} \\
\midrule
1 & Graph traversal & \texttt{nx.ancestors(G, partition\_id)} bounded by hop cap $\to$ collect upstream translations. \\
2 & SCC context & If \texttt{scc\_id} set: gather all sibling partitions in the same cycle. \\
3 & KB retrieval & \texttt{query\_similar(k=5, level="cluster", failure\_mode=...)}. Cluster level for macro families. \\
4 & Context merge & Graph context (upstream translations) $+$ KB pairs $+$ failure mode tags $\to$ combined prompt. \\
5 & Generate & Groq 70B (complexity warrants stronger model). \\
6 & Cross-verify & Second LLM call checks equivalence. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{Agentic RAG — Complex / High-Risk Blocks}

\begin{infobox}[When: MOD/HIGH risk, or any failure mode detected, or cross-verify fails on first attempt]
The agent \emph{autonomously controls the retrieval strategy}. It decides: (1) whether to retrieve at all, (2) how many results ($k$ adaptive), (3) which RAPTOR level to query, (4) whether to reformulate the query on failure, and (5) whether to escalate to graph-augmented context. Retrieval is retried — not just generation.
\end{infobox}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Agentic RAG Pipeline — Autonomous Retrieval Loop}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} c l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Step} & \textbf{Action} & \textbf{Details} \\
\midrule
1 & Decide whether to retrieve & UNCERTAIN $\to$ skip KB, emit stub. Otherwise proceed. \\
2 & Adaptive $k$ & Risk-driven: MOD $\to$ $k{=}5$, HIGH $\to$ $k{=}8$. \\
3 & Initial retrieval & \texttt{query\_similar(k, level="leaf", failure\_mode=detected\_mode)}. \\
4 & Generate & Groq 70B with failure mode tags $+$ KB pairs. \\
5 & Cross-verify & Second LLM checks equivalence. \\
6 & \textbf{Retrieval retry} (if cross-verify fails) & Reformulate query: append failure mode $+$ error description. \\
7 & Escalate $k$ and level & $k \mathrel{+}= 3$, switch \texttt{level="cluster"}. Re-retrieve. \\
8 & Re-generate & New context from escalated retrieval. \\
9 & Final escalation & If still failing: \texttt{level="root"} $+$ \texttt{nx.ancestors()} graph context (becomes GraphRAG+Agentic). \\
10 & Accept or PARTIAL & Pass $\to$ L4. All retries exhausted $\to$ mark PARTIAL, log for human review. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsubsection{RAG Paradigm Selection Summary}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{When Each RAG Paradigm Is Used}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\small
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l l l l l @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Paradigm} & \textbf{Trigger} & \textbf{Retrieval $k$} & \textbf{RAPTOR Level} & \textbf{Retry Strategy} \\
\midrule
Static RAG & LOW risk, no deps & 3 (fixed) & Leaf & None \\
GraphRAG & Cross-file deps / SCC & 5 (fixed) & Cluster & Generation only \\
Agentic RAG & MOD/HIGH risk or failure & 5$\to$8$\to$11 (adaptive) & Leaf$\to$Cluster$\to$Root & Retrieval + generation \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{infobox}[Key Distinction]
\textbf{Static RAG}: fixed retrieve $+$ generate. \textbf{GraphRAG}: graph-structured context injected before generation. \textbf{Agentic RAG}: the agent autonomously controls retrieval decisions — adaptive $k$, query reformulation, RAPTOR level escalation, and retrieval retry (not just generation retry). The three paradigms are complementary, selected per partition by StrategyAgent based on risk level and dependency structure.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Design Rationale}

The KB is the primary quality driver. Every LLM call is augmented with retrieved SAS$\to$Python/PySpark pairs. Built \textbf{without any human SAS expertise}: a 3-prompt dual-LLM chain (Prompt A $\to$ B $\to$ C cross-verify). Pairs with confidence $\geq 0.85$ are accepted as verified.

\begin{infobox}[KB Versioning]
Every KB example carries a \texttt{version} integer. Corrections create a new version; the old pair is retained with \texttt{superseded\_by} pointing to the replacement. A \texttt{kb\_changelog} DuckDB table logs every mutation (insert, update, supersede, rollback) with timestamp and author. Rollback: \texttt{python scripts/kb\_rollback.py --example\_id \{uuid\} --to\_version \{n\}}.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Why CodeBLEU, Not BLEU}

\begin{infobox}[CodeBLEU (Ren et al., 2020)]
BLEU was designed for natural language. For code, it fails because: (1) variable names don't need to match exactly, (2) it treats all tokens equally, (3) it ignores syntax structure. CodeBLEU fixes this with 4 weighted sub-scores:
\end{infobox}

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{CodeBLEU Sub-scores}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l c X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Sub-score} & \textbf{Weight} & \textbf{What It Measures} \\
\midrule
n-gram BLEU & 0.25 & Token-level overlap (surface similarity) \\
Weighted n-gram & 0.25 & Keyword-aware overlap (Python keywords weighted higher) \\
AST Match & 0.25 & Syntactic structure similarity (tree edit distance on ASTs) \\
Data-flow Match & 0.25 & Semantic equivalence via data-flow graph comparison \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{Composition Guarantee}

\begin{infobox}[Formal Composition Rule]
For any two consecutive agents $A_i$ and $A_{i+1}$: if $A_i$'s postcondition is satisfied, then $A_{i+1}$'s precondition is guaranteed to hold. This is enforced by:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Typed intermediate representations}: \texttt{PartitionIR}, \texttt{ConversionResult}, \texttt{RAPTORNode} are dataclasses with required fields — missing fields raise \texttt{ValidationError} before the next agent runs.
    \item \textbf{Schema enforcement}: SQLite tables have \texttt{NOT NULL} constraints; LanceDB vectors are dimension-checked ($= 768$); NetworkX nodes require \texttt{partition\_id}.
    \item \textbf{Quality gates}: Each agent's postcondition includes a quantitative target. If the target is not met, the retry policy activates (up to $n$ retries with fallback). If all retries fail, the block is marked \texttt{PARTIAL} or \texttt{FAILED} — it is \emph{never silently dropped}.
    \item \textbf{Monotonic progress}: Redis checkpoints guarantee that on crash recovery, no block is processed twice and no block is skipped. The invariant $|\text{output}| = |\text{input}|$ holds across restarts.
\end{enumerate}
\end{infobox}
