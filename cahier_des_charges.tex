\section{Context and Problem Statement}
% ══════════════════════════════════════════════════════════════

\subsection{Industrial Context}

Organizations operating on SAS face mounting pressure to migrate to modern, open-source Python stacks. The primary drivers are:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Migration Drivers}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Driver} & \textbf{Detail} \\
\midrule
Licensing cost & SAS enterprise licenses: \$8,000–\$100,000+ per seat/year. Python is free. \\
Ecosystem velocity & Python's ecosystem (pandas, scikit-learn, PyTorch, statsmodels) advances faster. \\
Talent availability & New graduates enter with Python skills, not SAS. \\
Infrastructure & Cloud-native architectures (AWS, GCP, Azure) are built around Python. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{infobox}[Scale of the Problem]
Manual translation costs 3–8 hours per 100 lines of SAS. For a 500,000-line codebase, this means \textbf{15,000–40,000 hours} of developer time — effectively years of effort.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Problem Statement — The Six Failure Modes}

The challenge is not syntactic translation — it is \textbf{semantic equivalence under complexity}. Six documented failure modes cause silent errors:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Six Critical Failure Modes in SAS$\to$Python Translation}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Failure Mode} & \textbf{Why It Breaks} \\
\midrule
\texttt{DATE\_ARITHMETIC} & SAS epoch: Jan 1, 1960 vs Python's 1970. Silent numerical errors in every date calculation. \\
\texttt{MERGE\_SEMANTICS} & SAS \texttt{MERGE} has sorted-join semantics fundamentally different from \texttt{pd.merge()}. \\
\texttt{RETAIN} & Creates persistent row-to-row state in DATA steps — no direct Python equivalent. \\
\texttt{FIRST./LAST.} & Automatic variables depend on BY-group processing absent in pandas. \\
\texttt{MISSING\_VALUE} & SAS numeric missing (\texttt{.}) behaves as $-\infty$ in comparisons; Python's \texttt{NaN} returns \texttt{False}. \\
\texttt{PROC\_MEANS\_OUTPUT} & \texttt{OUTPUT OUT=} produces a specific column structure that must be replicated exactly. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\subsection{Solution Approach}

An AI-powered, agentic SAS-to-Python conversion accelerator combining:

\begin{table}[H]
\centering
\caption{Core Approach Components}
\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.3}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Component} & \textbf{Description} \\
\midrule
Semantic partitioning & RAPTOR-inspired hierarchical clustering (Sarthi et al., ICLR 2024) instead of flat keyword splitting. \\
KB-augmented translation & Retrieve verified SAS$\to$Python/PySpark pairs as few-shot context, especially for failure modes. \\
Calibrated QA & Cross-verification by second LLM call; quality metrics tracked to detect drift. \\
Structured reconstruction & Merged in execution order with consolidated imports, resolved variables, syntax validation. \\
Continuous learning & Corrections feed back into the KB automatically. \\
\bottomrule
\end{tabularx}
\end{table}

\begin{table}[H]

\textbf{Stage} & \textbf{Component} & \textbf{Details} \\

6 & RAPTORTreeBuilder & Recursive: re-embed summaries, re-cluster. Stop: $k{=}1$ OR $\text{depth}{\geq}\text{max}$ OR $|\Delta\text{BIC}|<0.01$. \\

\subsection{Design Rationale}

The KB is the primary quality driver. Every LLM call is augmented with retrieved SAS$\to$Python/PySpark pairs. Built \textbf{without any human SAS expertise}: a 3-prompt dual-LLM chain (Prompt A $\to$ B $\to$ C cross-verify). Pairs with confidence $\geq 0.85$ are accepted as verified.

\begin{infobox}[KB Versioning]
Every KB example carries a \texttt{version} integer. Corrections create a new version; the old pair is retained with \texttt{superseded\_by} pointing to the replacement. A \texttt{kb\_changelog} DuckDB table logs every mutation (insert, update, supersede, rollback) with timestamp and author. Rollback: \texttt{python scripts/kb\_rollback.py --example\_id \{uuid\} --to\_version \{n\}}.
\end{infobox}

\subsection{Why CodeBLEU, Not BLEU}

\begin{infobox}[CodeBLEU (Ren et al., 2020)]
BLEU was designed for natural language. For code, it fails because: (1) variable names don't need to match exactly, (2) it treats all tokens equally, (3) it ignores syntax structure. CodeBLEU fixes this with 4 weighted sub-scores:
\end{infobox}

\renewcommand{\arraystretch}{1.25}
\begin{tabularx}{\textwidth}{@{} l c X @{}}
\toprule
\textbf{Sub-score} & \textbf{Weight} & \textbf{What It Measures} \\
\midrule
n-gram BLEU & 0.25 & Token-level overlap (surface similarity) \\
Weighted n-gram & 0.25 & Keyword-aware overlap (Python keywords weighted higher) \\
AST Match & 0.25 & Syntactic structure similarity (tree edit distance on ASTs) \\
Data-flow Match & 0.25 & Semantic equivalence via data-flow graph comparison \\
